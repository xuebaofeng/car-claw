2018 Hyundai Santa Fe Review

by Sarah Shelton | August 3, 2018

Available as either a 2-row or 3-row model, the 2018 Hyundai Santa Fe is a solid midsize SUV with a generally smooth ride, controlled handling, and great safety scores. However, it lacks first-rate qualities. For this reason, it sits in the bottom third of our midsize SUV rankings.

Is the Hyundai Santa Fe a Good SUV?

The Hyundai Santa Fe is a good SUV, but many rivals in the highly competitive midsize SUV class are better. It has great crash test scores, and its combination of quality and value made it a finalist for our 2018 Best 3-Row SUV for the Money award. The 2-row base model of the Santa Fe Sport is also a solid value, thanks to its more powerful optional turbocharged engine.

Should I Buy the Hyundai Santa Fe?

Shoppers on a tight budget will want to check out the 2018 Santa Fe Sport since it has one of the lowest standard prices in the midsize SUV class. It has a basic features list and engine, but it balances that with excellent safety test scores and one of the longest warranty lengths in the class. For roughly $6,000 more, you can get a more powerful vehicle with the turbocharged, five-seat Santa Fe Sport or the V6-powered, seven-seat Santa Fe. You’ll also get niceties like dual-zone climate control, Apple CarPlay, and Android Auto. Hyundai also has a new Shopper Assurance program that allows you to test drive the Santa Fe for an extended period of time. If you decide it's not the right vehicle for you, Hyundai will let you return it within three days.

The Santa Fe sits in the competitive midsize SUV segment with a plethora of worthy rivals. Before you buy, you may want to consider a few alternatives, such as the very roomy Volkswagen Atlas and the athletic Mazda CX-9.

Should I Buy a New or Used Hyundai Santa Fe?

There are no major changes for the 2018 Hyundai Santa Fe. The 2017 Santa Fe saw a mild refresh, which included updated exterior styling, a new standard 7-inch touch screen, and additional safety features. If you can do without these updates, you may want to consider very similar models from 2013 to 2016. Hyundai redesigned the Santa Fe for 2013, so models prior to that will look vastly different.

How Much Does the Hyundai Santa Fe Cost?

Prices for the 3-row Hyundai Santa Fe start at $30,850. In addition to the base SE, the Santa Fe also comes in the SE Ultimate ($38,850) and Limited Ultimate ($39,550) trim levels. Each comes standard with front-wheel drive, and you can add all-wheel drive for $1,750.

The smaller 2-row Hyundai Santa Fe Sport is also available in three trim levels: the base Santa Fe Sport ($24,950), the 2.0T ($31,350), and the 2.0T Ultimate ($35,650). Adding all-wheel drive to these models increases your price by $1,550.

Hyundai Santa Fe Versus the Competition

Which Is Better: Hyundai Santa Fe or Kia Sorento?

The Santa Fe and Kia Sorento have some things in common. Both come with either two or three rows and are built by the Korean Hyundai Motor Group. However, the Sorento has a higher-quality interior with available Nappa leather upholstery. A V6-equipped Sorento also costs less than a V6-equipped Santa Fe. Go for the Sorento.

Which Is Better: Hyundai Santa Fe or Honda Pilot?

Unlike the Santa Fe, the Honda Pilot has enough space for adults in its third row. If you plan on hauling a lot of people and their cargo, the Pilot’s roomy accommodations make it a great pick. The Honda also has a powerful V6 engine and excellent crash test scores. It's the superior option.

Which Is Better: Hyundai Santa Fe or Toyota Highlander?

Safety is a priority in the Toyota Highlander, which has a generous list of active safety features. Among its standard equipment is adaptive cruise control and lane departure warning (optional in the Santa Fe). The Highlander costs more than the Santa Fe, but its good resale value makes it the better vehicle.

Santa Fe Interior

How Many People Does the Santa Fe Seat?

You can carry a party of five in the Santa Fe Sport. The larger Santa Fe adds a third-row seat, expanding your crew by three. The exception is the Santa Fe Limited Ultimate – this swaps out the second-row bench for two captain's chairs, capping your capacity at six. The Santa Fe is ready for spills, with standard stain resistant cloth seats that welcome juice boxes and sticky treats. Most people will find the seats comfortable, but some may think they're too firm.

Santa Fe and Car Seats

The Santa Fe and Santa Fe Sport come with the same amount of LATCH anchors: three tether anchors and two sets of lower anchors, all located in the second row. The hardware is mostly easy to use, though locating the upper anchors and accessing the lower anchors take a little extra effort.

Santa Fe Interior Quality

The Santa Fe receives a B for its interior styling and quality. Most features are nice, and you'll get premium touches like leather seats and upgraded trim accents as you move up the trim levels. There are some lower grade elements, though, and not everyone is thrilled with the design.

Santa Fe Cargo Space

Cargo space is limited inside the Santa Fe. Behind the third row is a 13.5-cubic-foot cubby – more than half a dozen 3-row rivals can top that. The 40.9 cubic feet behind the second-row seat (35.4 cubic feet in the Santa Fe Sport) is also below average.

Santa Fe Infotainment, Bluetooth, and Navigation

Every Santa Fe comes with six speakers, a USB port, a 5-inch infotainment display, and a Bluetooth connection for phone functions. On the Santa Fe Sport, we recommend upgrading to the 7-inch display, which also comes with Apple CarPlay and Android Auto (this is the base infotainment system in the 3-row Santa Fe). Navigation, an Infinity premium audio system, and USB ports in the third row are also available.

Santa Fe Performance

Santa Fe Engine: Pick the Turbo 4 in the Sport

Pick the turbocharged engine if you buy the 2-row Santa Fe Sport, unless you don't mind an SUV that takes its time getting up to speed. The 3-row Santa Fe comes with a V6. Both the turbo four and V6 are satisfying, with the higher horsepower V6 engine offering the most grunt of the two. With it, you can tow up to 5,000 pounds (up from the Santa Fe Sport 2.0's 3,500 pounds).

Santa Fe Gas Mileage: Good Fuel Economy

With any of the three available engines, the Santa Fe returns favorable fuel economy ratings. The base Santa Fe Sport trim is the most fuel-efficient: It gets 21 mpg in the city and 27 mpg on the highway. The Santa Fe Sport's turbocharged engine is rated at up to 20/28 mpg city/highway. The Santa Fe's V6 gets an EPA-estimated 18/25 mpg.

Santa Fe Ride and Handling: Quiet and Mostly Smooth

The Santa Fe has a nicely weighted steering wheel and feels stable on the road. Feedback on its ride quality is mixed, with some, but not all, test drivers finding the ride overly firm. Every Santa Fe comes with Drive Mode Select, which lets you choose from Eco, Sport, and Normal driving modes.

Santa Fe Reliability

Is the Hyundai Santa Fe Reliable?

The 2018 Santa Fe receives a slightly above-average predicted reliability score of 3.5 out of five from J.D. Power.

Hyundai Santa Fe Warranty

The 2018 Santa Fe comes with a five-year/60,000-mile warranty and a 10-year/100,000-mile powertrain warranty.

Santa Fe Safety

Santa Fe Crash Test Results

The Insurance Institute for Highway Safety (IIHS) awards the 2018 Santa Fe the highest rating of Good in five crash tests and an Acceptable rating in the small overlap front passenger-side test. Both the Santa Fe and Santa Fe Sport have 2018 Top Safety Pick+ designations from the IIHS for their Superior ability to prevent front crashes when equipped with the available automatic emergency braking system. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration also awards the Santa Fe and Santa Fe Sport perfect five-star overall safety ratings.

Santa Fe Safety Features

Every Santa Fe comes with a rearview camera. Available safety features include lane change assist, blind spot detection, rear cross traffic alert, a multi-view camera, rear parking sensors, adaptive cruise control, lane departure warning, automatic high beams, adaptive headlights, and automatic emergency braking with pedestrian detection.

Which Hyundai Santa Fe Model Is Right for Me?

Size and capacity quickly distinguish Hyundai's fraternal midsize twins. The smaller of the two is the Santa Fe Sport. This 2-row SUV can carry up to five people. It has less cargo space but a lower starting price. The base model starts at around $25,000 and comes with stain-resistant cloth seats and a respectable set of standard technology. Two additional trim levels are available for the Santa Fe Sport, but if you can afford a higher price tag, we recommend upgrading to the 3-row Santa Fe instead. You can haul two extra people on your adventure, and with its V6 engine, your towing capacity jumps to 5,000 pounds – more than double that of the base Sport model. This well-equipped SUV also features a larger infotainment display that includes Apple CarPlay and Android Auto, yet its $30,850 sticker price is lower than the midlevel Santa Fe Sport 2.0T (MSRP: $31,350).

Hyundai Santa Fe Sport

The 2-row Santa Fe Sport ($24,950) has a 185-horsepower four-cylinder engine, a six-speed automatic transmission, and front-wheel drive. Standard features include a six-speaker sound system, a 5-inch infotainment display, Bluetooth connectivity for phone functions, a USB port, a rearview camera, and a 40/20/40 split-folding second-row bench seat. Like all Santa Fe Sport trims, all-wheel drive is available for $1,550.

Hyundai Santa Fe Sport 2.0T

The 240-horsepower Santa Fe Sport ($31,350) not only features a more powerful four-cylinder engine, but it also comes packed with goodies. Occupants can unwind with its leather seats, leather-wrapped shifting knob and steering wheel, heated front seats, manual rear side window sunshades, eight-way power-adjustable driver's seat, and six-way power-adjustable front passenger seat. Also included are a 7-inch infotainment display, Apple CarPlay, Android Auto, HD Radio, satellite radio, dual-zone automatic climate control, a proximity key, push-button start, lane change assist, blind spot detection, rear cross traffic alert, and a hands-free smart liftgate with auto open.

Hyundai Santa Fe SE

The non-Sport Santa Fe comes with three rows of seats, a 290-horsepower 3.3-liter V6 engine, a six-speed automatic transmission, and front-wheel drive. Adding all-wheel drive to any 3-row Santa Fe costs $1,750. The base Santa Fe ($30,850) has seating for seven, cloth upholstery, an eight-way power-adjustable driver's seat, and a 40/20/40 split-folding second-row bench seat. Other standard features include dual-zone automatic climate control, a 7-inch infotainment display, Bluetooth connectivity for phone functions, a USB port, Android Auto, Apple CarPlay, satellite radio, HD Radio, a six-speaker sound system, and a rearview camera.

Hyundai Santa Fe SE Ultimate

Notable features in the midlevel Santa Fe SE Ultimate include leather upholstery, heated and ventilated front seats, heated second-row seats, an 8-inch touch screen, navigation, a panoramic sunroof, a household-style power outlet, USB ports in the third row, a hands-free smart liftgate with auto open, a multi-view camera, blind spot monitoring, rear cross traffic alert, lane change assist, and rear parking sensors. Prices start at $38,850.

Hyundai Santa Fe Limited Ultimate

The $39,550 Limited Ultimate is the only six-seat configuration in the lineup. It replaces the second-row bench seat with a pair of captain's chairs.

The Final Call

Overall, the Hyundai Santa Fe and Hyundai Santa Fe Sport are decent choices for SUV shoppers. With several seating configurations, engines, and interior packages to select from, you have some flexibility in building the Santa Fe that fits your lifestyle. However, this category is packed with outstanding SUVs, including the Toyota Highlander, Kia Sorento, and Volkswagen Atlas. You can see how these compete in our midsize SUV rankings, and compare them side by side with our Car Comparison Tool.
